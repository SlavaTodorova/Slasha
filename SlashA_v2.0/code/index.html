WhatIs

What is it?

This is SLASH/A N-gram Tendency Viewer, our project for the Visualization seminar led by Dr Chris Culy at Universität Tübingen in the winter semester 2013/2014.

Our goal was to develop a visualization tool for the exploration of the tendencies in the change of language over time.

Slash/A can visualize the n-grams frequencies from any correspondence corpus (given the corpus in the right format) and provides the user with the option of choosing among several levels of abstraction to see general tendencies out of raw data.

Who are we?

We are Maria Chinkina and Velislava Todorova.

We are Master students in Computational Linguistics at Universität Tübingen.

Why would one use this tool?

Slash/A makes it possible to compare the changes or the tendencies in the use of language over time across different subsets for which your corpus is annotated.

You can compare authors, recipients, genres, topics – we will make the most of every annotation that you have in your corpus. All you need to do is to configure the Settings, load your corpus and start to explore.

HowTo

Where do I start?


  • Configure the corpus settings by clicking on in the top-right corner of the page.

  • Load (part of) your corpus in XML format. (the Browinings' corpus is provided as an example)

  • Type in one or several tokens to search in the whole corpus or only in the letters from/to some senders/addressees.
    You may look for any token – words as well as punctuation marks.
A valid UNI-GRAM search would be:

Simple:

love

Lemmas:

love/lemma

love/

POS tags:

love/vbp
(all non 3rd person singular forms of the verb)

love/v*
(all forms of the verb)

Lemmas & POS tags:

love/lemma/nnp*
(for all the proper nouns (singular and plural) that are forms of the lemma 'love')


A valid N-GRAM search would be:

i love your verses

i love your *s/nns
(for all sequences of the form 'i love your [any noun in plural ending with -s]')

i love/lemma you

i love/v* you
(for all sequences of the form 'i [any form of the verb love] you')


A valid POS search would be:

/vbp
(for all verbal non third person singular present forms)

/v*
(for all verbal forms)


  • Hit the “Show Graph” button. A line representing the frequencies of each word will appear.

    The darker the background behind the line, the more data there is on this day.

  • Click on the smoothing level buttons to see the tendencies of the word usage.

Is there any other functionality?

  • Go to "Add some statistics?" to display significant fluctuations (for each n-gram) or significant difference (for the same n-gram searched for in 2 different subsets).

  • Mouse over the dots on the graph to see the detailed information about the data for each day.

  • Click on the dots on the graph to display the original text with highlighted occurrences of an n-gram on a particular day.

  • Click “show/hide data points” to show/hide the dots on the graph.

  • Click on the n-gram label at the end of the line to remove it.
    You can restore it later.

  • Scroll down to the bottom of the page to see the history of your searches.

    Under the “Removed” tab one can find the recently removed graphs. Each of them can be restored with a click.

    The complete history is given under the “Successful” tab.

    The n-grams that were not found in the corpus are under the "Not Found" tab.

Additional Options

You can use the wildcard * at any position in the POS-tag to perform a more general search.

Here are some example queries:
N* Noun
V* Verb
PP* Pronoun
JJ* Adjective
RB* Adverb
W* Wh-word
NNP* Any Proper Noun (singular or plural)
N*S Any Noun (common or proper) in plural
